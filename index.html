Hekmat Bechir Fathallah Antaki

1923 – 1993

Biography

Hekmat Bechir Fathallah Antaki was an Egyptian organic chemist. He completed his doctoral degree at Queen Mary College, University of London, in 1950, under the supervision of J.R. Partington. He returned to Egypt and joined the Research Institute for Tropical Medicine, Cairo, where he conducted an independent programme of research in heterocyclic chemistry between 1951 and 1967. He subsequently served as Director of the Research Institute of Medical Entomology, Cairo.

He published eight papers in four leading chemistry journals between 1951 and 1967. Working without university affiliation, he developed multicomponent condensation methods for the synthesis of pharmacologically relevant heterocyclic scaffolds. His 1962 paper was submitted from his home address in Agouza, Cairo — self-funded research by a working scientist.

Structural Correction: Resolving a Forty-Year Error

In 1911, Palazzo and Tamburini obtained the first representative of the 4-oxo-4H-pyrido[1,2-a]pyrimidine series from 2-aminopyridine and ethyl acetoacetate, but assigned it the wrong structure. The error persisted through Seide (1925) and Crippa and Scevola (1937), who also preferred the incorrect 2-oxo-2H formulation. For forty years, the wrong structure circulated in the chemical literature.

In 1951, working at Queen Mary College London under J. R. Partington, Hekmat Antaki and V. Petrow resolved the question unambiguously. They developed an independent synthesis — reacting 2-bromopyridine with ethyl β-aminocrotonate — and demonstrated that the product was identical to the compound obtained by the acetoacetate route, proving the structure was the 4-keto isomer, not the 2-keto isomer as previously believed.

This correction was subsequently confirmed by Adams and Pachter (1952), by Shur and Israelstam at the University of Witwatersrand (Journal of Organic Chemistry, 1968, 33, 3015), and was comprehensively documented by Hermecz and Mészáros in their canonical 1983 review in Advances in Heterocyclic Chemistry (Vol. 33, Academic Press, pp. 251, 256), which identifies Antaki and Petrow's 1951 paper as the definitive structural proof. Hermecz further notes, at p. 319, that misassignment of these compounds as 2-oxo-2H isomers or as 1,8-naphthyridines continued in the literature even after 1951 — making the correction both necessary and consequential.

Ultraviolet Spectroscopy: A Priority Record

Between 1957 and 1962, working from the Research Institute for Tropical Medicine in Cairo, Hekmat Antaki established the foundational ultraviolet absorption record for the pyrido[1,2-a]pyrimidine compound class. His findings, published in the Journal of the American Chemical Society in 1958 (Vol. 80, p. 3066), reported the first systematic UV spectra for a series of 4H-pyrido[1,2-a]pyrimidine derivatives in absolute ethanol, including full spectral curves and extinction coefficients. His 1962 paper in the Journal of Organic Chemistry (Vol. 27, p. 1371) provided the first correct mechanistic interpretation, attributing the band at λmax ~245 mμ to the —C=C—C=O chromophore of the pyrimidine moiety, and the band in the range 300–400 nm to the 2-imino-1-substituted-1,2-dihydropyridine chromophore.

Both attributions were independently confirmed by Shur and Israelstam (Journal of Organic Chemistry, 1968, 33, 3016), who cited Antaki's 1958 and 1962 papers directly for these assignments, and were comprehensively documented by Hermecz and Mészáros in their 1983 canonical review (Advances in Heterocyclic Chemistry, Vol. 33, Academic Press, p. 319), which explicitly states that Antaki established the chromophore assignments for both absorption bands characteristic of this compound class.

This spectroscopic framework remains the reference point for current research into the fluorescence and photophysical properties of pyrido[1,2-a]pyrimidine derivatives.
